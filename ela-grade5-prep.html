📄 Passage — Fiction

    Maya had lived next to the old lighthouse her entire life, but she had never once climbed its spiral staircase. The lighthouse keeper, Mr. Holloway, was a quiet man who rarely spoke to anyone in town. People whispered that he had sealed himself inside after losing his fishing boat in the great storm of 1987.


    One gray November afternoon, Maya found the lighthouse door swinging open. She stepped inside and called out, but heard only her own echo. The stairs wound upward like a sleeping snake. What harm could there be in one small peek? she thought. As she climbed, photographs lined the stone walls—Mr. Holloway laughing with a crew of fishermen, a wedding portrait, a little boy blowing out birthday candles.


    At the top, the great lamp sat cold and dark. Maya pressed her face to the glass and saw the whole village below—the crooked rooftops, the silver ribbon of the river, and her own tiny house with its red door. Then she heard slow footsteps on the stairs.


    "I haven't been up here in twelve years," Mr. Holloway said, not unkindly. He looked at the photographs as if seeing old friends. "After the storm, I locked the door because I thought forgetting would hurt less." He paused. "I was wrong."


    Maya didn't say anything. She simply stood beside him as they watched the sun break through the clouds and scatter gold across the water.

📄 Passage — Informational Text

The Problem with Plastic Straws


    Every day, Americans use approximately 500 million plastic straws. Many of these straws end up in oceans and waterways, where they harm marine life. Sea turtles, for example, often mistake straws for food, which can block their digestive systems and lead to death.


    Unlike aluminum cans or glass bottles, plastic straws are too lightweight to be sorted by recycling machines. As a result, most straws end up in landfills or are swept into the ocean. Once in the ocean, plastic breaks into tiny pieces called microplastics, which can enter the food chain when fish eat them.


    Several cities and countries have already banned single-use plastic straws. In 2018, Seattle became the first major U.S. city to do so. Many restaurants now offer paper straws, bamboo straws, or stainless steel reusable straws as alternatives. Critics of the ban argue that people with certain disabilities rely on flexible plastic straws and that alternatives are not always effective.


    Scientists agree that reducing plastic pollution is essential to protecting ocean ecosystems. While straws represent only a small percentage of ocean plastic, raising awareness about them can encourage people to rethink their everyday plastic use more broadly.

📌 Strong Opinion Paragraph Checklist:

  • Clear opinion statement (claim) in the first sentence
  • At least 2 reasons with evidence or examples
  • Linking words: because, therefore, for example, in addition, as a result
  • Concluding sentence that restates your opinion
