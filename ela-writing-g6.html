ELAGSE6W1 · Argument Writing
⚔️
Argument Writing
Make a strong claim, support it with evidence from sources, AND address the opposing viewpoint. This is the #1 upgrade from Grade 5 Opinion writing.
⬆️
Grade 5 → 6 Upgrade: In Grade 5, you just gave your opinion and reasons. In Grade 6, you must also acknowledge the other side (counterclaim) and explain why your argument is still stronger. This is called a rebuttal.
📝 Your Argument Prompt
Some people argue that middle school students should not be allowed to use cell phones during school hours. Others believe that phones are useful learning tools. Write an argument essay for or against cell phone use in school. Include a counterclaim and rebuttal. Support your position with at least two pieces of evidence.

ELAGSE6W2 · Research / Informational Writing
🔬
Research Writing
Write an informational essay using multiple sources. You must summarize, paraphrase, and cite your sources — no copying!
⬆️
Grade 5 → 6 Upgrade: You must now reference where your information comes from. Use phrases like "According to National Geographic..." or "A 2023 study found that..." instead of just stating facts.
📝 Your Research Prompt
Using the source information provided below, write an informational essay explaining how climate change affects ocean ecosystems. Use information from at least two of the sources. Cite your sources by referring to them by name in your writing.

📂 Sources to Use

Source A — NOAA (National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration): Ocean temperatures have risen by about 0.13°C per decade since 1901. Warmer waters cause coral bleaching, which has damaged over 50% of the world's coral reefs. Coral reefs support about 25% of all marine species.
Source B — National Geographic, 2023: As oceans absorb CO₂ from the atmosphere, they become more acidic — a process called ocean acidification. This makes it harder for shellfish, crabs, and corals to build their shells. Scientists warn that acidification could threaten the entire ocean food chain.
Source C — WWF (World Wildlife Fund): Sea level rise caused by melting ice threatens coastal ecosystems like mangroves and salt marshes, which are nursery habitats for many fish species. Without these habitats, fish populations that humans depend on for food could collapse.

ELAGSE6W9 · Literary Analysis
📚
Literary Analysis Writing
Write analytically about a story or poem. Explain theme, character development, or author's craft — and back up every claim with text evidence (quotes).
⭐
Brand New in Grade 6: Literary Analysis is writing about what you read. You pick a theme or idea, make a claim about it, and prove it using quotes from the text. This skill is used all the way through high school and college.
📝 Your Literary Analysis Prompt
Read the passage below, then write a literary analysis essay explaining how the author develops the theme that "courage means acting despite fear." Use at least two pieces of text evidence (direct quotes) to support your analysis.

📖 Passage: "The Storm" (excerpt)

Maya stood at the edge of the cliff, her knees trembling. Below, the river churned white and angry. Her brother was down there somewhere, carried by the current after the bridge collapsed. "I can't," she whispered. But then she remembered what her grandmother always said: "The brave ones aren't the ones who aren't afraid. They're the ones who jump anyway." Maya closed her eyes. She thought of her brother's laugh, the way he always saved her the last piece of pizza, how he'd once climbed a tree to rescue her kite without being asked. She opened her eyes. The river roared. She jumped.
Three hours later, wrapped in a rescue blanket, Maya couldn't stop shaking — but not from cold. "Were you scared?" the paramedic asked. She almost laughed. "Terrified," she said. "Still am." He raised an eyebrow. "Then why'd you do it?" Maya looked at her brother sleeping in the cot beside her, his chest rising and falling. "Because being scared wasn't a good enough reason not to."

Georgia Grade 6 Writing Rubric

Same 4 categories as Grade 5 — but the expectations are higher. Each category is worth 1–4 points. Know these and you know exactly what your teacher is looking for.

💡 Ideas & Content

Is your claim or thesis clear? Do you have specific evidence? Does everything connect to your main idea?

4
Strong thesis; specific, relevant evidence throughout
3
Clear idea; mostly supported with some gaps
2
Idea present but lacks specifics or evidence
1
Unclear or off-topic

🗂️ Organization

Clear intro → body → conclusion? Effective transitions? Does the structure support your argument?

4
Strong structure; transitions guide the reader naturally
3
Mostly organized; minor issues with flow
2
Some structure but choppy or out of order
1
Difficult to follow

🎨 Style & Voice

Appropriate formal tone? Varied sentence structures? Strong academic vocabulary?

4
Formal, engaging; varied sentences; strong word choice
3
Appropriate tone; some variety
2
Flat or informal; repetitive phrasing
1
Very basic; inappropriate tone

✏️ Conventions

Grammar, spelling, punctuation, capitalization, complete sentences?

4
Very few errors; easy and clear to read
3
Some errors but does not impede understanding
2
Frequent errors; sometimes distracting
1
Errors make it hard to understand
⬆️ Grade 5 vs Grade 6: What's Different?
Category Grade 5 Grade 6
Opinion/Argument State opinion + 2 reasons + conclusion Claim + evidence + counterclaim + rebuttal
Informational Explain a topic with facts Research-based; cite sources by name
Narrative Story with beginning/middle/end More complex plot structure; deeper character development
NEW: Literary Analysis Not required Thesis + text evidence + analysis of author's craft
Length 150–250 words 300–450 words
Vocabulary Grade-appropriate Academic/formal vocabulary expected
💡 Quick Tips by Writing Type

⚔️ Argument

  • Open with a surprising statistic
  • Each body ¶ = 1 reason + evidence
  • Counterclaim: "Some argue that..."
  • Rebuttal: "However, evidence shows..."
  • Avoid "I think" — say "Evidence suggests"

🔬 Research

  • Always name your source in the sentence
  • Paraphrase, never copy
  • Use: "According to... / [Source] states..."
  • Link sources with transitions
  • End with why the topic matters

📚 Literary Analysis

  • Thesis = claim about theme/character
  • Quote → Explain → Connect
  • Use literary present tense
  • Phrases: "This reveals... / suggests... / demonstrates..."
  • Don't summarize — analyze!
